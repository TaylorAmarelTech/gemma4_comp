(2.) Overview of the scheme


Facebook, the most ubiquitous social media platform in the world, is probably the largest facilitator of human trafficking and money laundering. Facebook is facilitating human trafficking and money laundering as it is taking insufficient action against these risks and not adhering to appropriate laws and policies. Worse, Facebook is not following its own guidelines and has not appropriately disclosed the proliferation of these activities to its shareholders contrary to SEC regulations as well as its permissive role and the associated risks.

Case Example
        Human Trafficking
Facebook is used by many human traffickers posing as employment agencies for sourcing foreign domestic workers for employers in Hong Kong SAR. There exists hundreds of thousands of Facebook pages, Facebook groups, and Facebook profiles that are obtusely used for the purposes of human trafficking, money laundering, and exploitation of migrants. Promises of employment and a just wage are promised for exorbitant employment and processing fees are charged to both employee and employer, leading to the employee being referred to predatory loan providers who charge illegal interest fees of up to 300% which in turn lead to employees working in indebted servitude: better known as slavery. Moreover, employees are rendered powerless as their passports are used as collateral for their loans, which is another illegal practice.


Money Laundering
One of many examples covered by this binder is the case of HOYA Lending Investor Corp, a money lender in the Philippines granting loans to prospective overseas foreign workers who were  referred to Hoya for a loan as a result of being charged excessive fees by employment agencies. These loans carried excessive interest rates under Philippines and Hong Kong SAR laws, and victims were not told the true interest rate or cost of the loan. These loan monies thus constituted proceeds of an indictable offence under Hong Kong SAR law, therefore amounting to money laundering. This was the same for Philippines law as the loans were deceptive. Virtually all communication - from advertising and recruiting to communication and harassment - were conducted on Facebook. Yet no action was taken on their part at any point to help the victim or address the prevalent and persistent abuses and the human trafficking and money laundering taking place. 


Non-Disclosure
Worse, it is suggested that Facebook has not made the appropriate disclosures of these risks to their shareholders despite knowing the extent of these activities occurring on their platform. Facebook is therefore being used by HOYA as a platform to attract and deceive victims into situations that are internationally recognised  as human trafficking. Facebook is then used to further exploit, harass and coerce the victims of human trafficking, often resorting to threatening their families and releasing their personal data online.


Other similar cases include the Grand Fusion case, Mega Lending Resources Corp case, Yoursun Lending Company case, Nittan Capital Finance Corporation case and Tamas Employment Agency case.


Precedent
Facebook has previously been held liable for its failure to help protect against teen sex trafficking by a Texan court, with the landmark finding that it can be held liable for omissions outside of the section 230 protections for tech companies. In a recent 2021 case, Facebook was also held liable for human trafficking connections by the Texas Supreme Court. As explained, "Holding internet platforms accountable for the words or actions of their users is one thing... holding internet platforms accountable for their own misdeeds is quite another thing. This is particularly the case for human trafficking."[1]This evidence that Facebook can definitely be held liable for their omissions in regards to the failure to address the human trafficking and money laundering being facilitated on their platform.


There is also a chance that - like its role in the sale of endangered wildlife parts on its site[2] - Facebook is knowingly profiting from the sex trafficking and this would be a motive for the lack of action taken on their part. This has previously drawn a complaint to the Securities and Exchange Commission for investigation. 


Because of the scale of the issue and its impact Facebook - as a public company - has obligations to disclose truthful and accurate information to shareholders. Furthermore, Facebook can not omit materially important information from its disclosures and SEC filings. A review of Facebook SEC filings has revealed that despite ubiquitous and public concern on the subject of human trafficking facilitated by Facebook, Facebook has decided to do the minimum and has disclosed information largely related to sex trafficking rather than human trafficking. Moreover, a consortium of Facebook insiders and critics have reportedly already filed a confidential whistleblower's complaint to the Securities and Exchange Commission, alleging that the company is already aware of the illegal activity. Further evidence is expected with the unveiling of the Facebook files on the Wall Street journal, which alleges that Facebook has closely studied how human traffickers use the platform to ensnare victims and advertise illegal sex services. The documents show that despite their findings Facebook took inadequate or no action at all.


This is grounds for complaint to and investigation by the Securities and Exchange Commission. Facilitating illegal acts via a platform and profiting from it means that failing to disclose this type of risk of abuse to Facebook's shareholders is a violation of the Securities and Exchange Commission regulations governing publicly traded companies.

(3.) Human Trafficking Activity on Facebook
Human Trafficking:


Human Trafficking, the trapping and exploitation of a person using deception, violence, or coercion, also known as modern slavery comes in many shapes and sizes harming adults and children in countries rich and poor alike harming millions of people globally and earns traffickers around hundreds of billions annually. 


Human trafficking is most prevalent in industries that are under-regulated, that rely on low-skilled or unskilled labor, and in which competition drives companies to seek cheaper solutions. Globalization has increased those pressures and brought them to more corners of the world. Human Trafficking generally takes three main forms: forced labor (which includes sex trafficking), forced marriage, and forced organ removal. Forced labor is the most common form of human trafficking affecting nearly twenty five million people. It most often takes place in the private sector industries such as domestic work in which individuals are trapped in bonded labor, they are obligated to work until they have repaid a loan or inherited debts in exploitative situations. 


Scale of Human Trafficking and Negative Impact:


The effects of human trafficking go beyond the 40.3 million people[3] that are directly affected by it. This issue undermines global peace and security by bankrolling criminal organizations, it threatens human rights, it weakens the global economy by functioning as an illicit multibillion dollar industry and undercuts global development by uprooting and destabilizing communities.


Facebook as a Medium for Human Trafficking:


As technology developed throughout the past years, industries started digitizing their processes to increase efficiency and reduce costs, the human trafficking industry, altough an illicit industry, didn´t fail to adapt. It started using social media as one of its main enabling tools to increase its reach and grow its activities. Facebook, the most widely used social media platform, has become one of the major digital mediums where these illicit operations are conducted[4]. Through user pages or group pages on Facebook both official and unofficial recruitment agencies attract foriegn domestic workers (FDW) by advertising deceitful employment opportunities. Once reeled in through Facebook, these foreign domestic workers are often charged illegal placement fees which they are unable to pay[5]. They are then manipulated through facebook chats and other mediums into taking a loan from money lenders, who are often partners of the same employment agencies, to cover these expenses. The passports of the FDW are used as collateral to secure these loans which usually charge illegal amounts of interest rates. Oftentimes the employment agencies even confiscate the passports and hold on to them until the loans have been repaid in full. FDW are also harassed by these traffickers via Facebook posts which release private confidential information or even threaten these workers and their families to coerce them into paying the loans or keep working. These foreign domestic workers are consequently trapped in bonded labor and obligated to work under inhuman conditions in exploitative situations. 




Conclusion:


In Hong Kong SAR foreign domestic workers who comprise 5% of the total population of the territory (380,000) as well as 10% of the workforce are a significant target of modern slavery which is enabled through Facebook´s services . Although carrying an important social role by helping the needs of child and elder day care in Hong Kong SAR for an affordable price, FDW are subject to serious violations of human and labour rights. The treatment of foreign domestic workers is a consequence of the laws and policies in place in Hong Kong SAR, as well as the lack of adequate actions from the Government to enforce domestic legislation to protect FDWs from trafficking, exploitation and forced labour which are strongly enabled by social platforms such as Facebook.

a. Taiwan Human Trafficking Prevention and Control Act
	Summary of Law:


The Human Trafficking Prevention Act is enacted to prevent human trafficking and to safeguard the rights of victims. The Act was promulgated in January 2009 and has been amended in May 2016.


The Act focuses on the obligations of the governments of municipalities and counties (cities) to hold regularly liaison and coordination conferences on human trafficking prevention and control. Furthermore, designate authorities or departments should specifically integrate to fulfill functions such as the implementation of the human trafficking prevention and control policies, the investigation of human trafficking cases, referral of criminal cases, the safeguards of the rights of victims, etc. 


Chapter Two focuses on the prevention and identification for the competent authorities on training, identification of victims and setting up of special channels or telephone hotlines for the reporting of human trafficking. 


Chapter Three focuses on the protection of victims such as providing medical treatment, protection and sheltering, legal assistance, psychological advice and counseling, etc.


Chapter Four sets out the penalties for the offenders of the Act.


(Source: https://law.moj.gov.tw/ENG/LawClass/LawAll.aspx?pcode=D0080177) 


Key Provisions:


                                                * Article 2 - Definitions


1. Human trafficking:

(1) To recruit, trade, take into bondage, transport, deliver, receive, harbor, hide, broker, or accommodate a local or foreign person, by force, threat, intimidation, confinement, monitoring, drugs, hypnosis, fraud, purposeful concealment of important information, illegal debt bondage, withholding important documents, making use of the victim’s inability, ignorance or helplessness, or by other means against his/her will, for the intention of subjecting him/her to sexual transactions, labor to which pay is not commensurate with the work duty, organ harvesting; or to use the above-mentioned means to impose sexual transactions, labor to which pay is not commensurate with the work duty, or organ harvesting on the victims.

(2) To recruit, trade, take into bondage, transport, deliver, receive, harbor, hide, broker, to sex transactions, labor to which pay is not commensurate with the work duty, or organ harvesting, or to subject people under 18 years of age to sexual transactions, labor to which pay is not commensurate with the work duty, or organ harvesting.


2. Offenses of human trafficking:
Referring to acts of human trafficking conducted in violation of this Act, the Criminal Code, Labor Standard Law, or Child and Youth Sexual Exploitation Prevention Act, or other related laws.


3. Improper debt bondage:
Referring to the use of unclear contracts or unreasonable payments of a debt to place people under bondage, subjecting them to sexual transactions, labor exploitation, or organ harvesting in order to fulfill or guarantee the payment of their debts.


(Source: https://law.moj.gov.tw/ENG/LawClass/LawAll.aspx?pcode=D0080177) 


Principal Offenders and Joint Offenders:


Articles 31-32, 34: Principal Offenders are those that commit human trafficking, whereby use of force, threat, intimidation, confinement, montiring, drugs, fraud, hypnosis or others mean against another person’s will to labor is involved. Any attempt to commit either of the crimes stated in Article 32 is punishable.


Articles 33-34: Joint Offenders are those that help recruit, deliver, receive, harbour, hide, broker or accommodate another minor in order to subject him/her to labor exploitation or underpayment for profit. Any attempt of this crime is punishable. 


(Source: https://law.moj.gov.tw/ENG/LawClass/LawAll.aspx?pcode=D0080177)

“Taiwan is host to approximately 714,000 foreign workers, most of whom are hired in their home countries through recruitment agencies and brokers – including some from Taiwan – to perform low-skilled work as home caregivers and domestic workers (34 percent), or in farming, manufacturing, meat processing, construction, and fishing. Taiwan Criminal Code 


Article 296-1 
A person who trades in or mortgages humans shall be sentenced to imprisonment for not less than five years and, in addition thereto, a fine of not more than five hundred thousand yuan may be imposed.

A person who for purpose to make a person to engage in sexual intercourse or to make an obscene act commits the offense specified in the preceding paragraph shall be sentenced to imprisonment for less than seven years and, in addition thereto, a fine of not more than five hundred thousand yuan may be imposed.

A person who through the use of violence, threats, intimidation, controls, drugs, hypnosis or another means commit an offense specified in the two preceding paragraphs shall be sentenced to a punishment by increasing it up to one half.

A person who arranges, accepts, conceals a traded or mortgaged person specified in the three preceding paragraphs or to cause him to conceal shall be sentenced to imprisonment for not less than one year but not more than seven years, in addition thereto, a fine of not more than three hundred thousand yuan may be imposed.

A public official who harbors a person who commits an offense specified in the four preceding paragraphs shall be sentenced to the punishment prescribed in the relating paragraph by increasing it by one half.

An attempt to commit an offense specified in paragraphs 1 to 3 is punishable.


Article 302
A person who without authority takes another into custody or by other illegal means deprives him of his freedom of movement shall be sentenced to imprisonment for not more than five years, short-term imprisonment, or a fine of not more than nine thousand dollars.

If death results from the commission of the offense, the offender shall be sentenced to life imprisonment or imprisonment for not less than seven years; if aggravated injury results, the offender shall be sentenced to imprisonment for not less than three years but not more than ten years.

An attempt to commit an offense specified in paragraph 1 is punishable.


Chapter 28 Offenses Against Privacy


Article 335
A person who has lawful possession of property belonging to another and who takes it for purpose to exercise unlawful control over it for himself or for a third person shall be sentenced to imprisonment for not more than five years or short-term imprisonment; in lieu thereof, or in addition thereto, a fine of not more than thirty thousand dollars may be imposed.


An attempt to commit an offense specified in the preceding paragraph is punishable.


(Source: https://law.moj.gov.tw/ENG/LawClass/LawAll.aspx?pcode=C0000001) 


Article 15 Taiwan Criminal Code
A person who has a legal obligation and is able to prevent the results of the occurrence of an offense but has failed to do so shall be equal to have caused the occurrence of the result by his positive act.

If a conduct of a person causes the danger of producing the result of an offense, the person has a legal obligation to prevent the occurrence of the result.


(Source: https://law.moj.gov.tw/ENG/LawClass/LawAll.aspx?pcode=C0000001)

a. Hong Kong SAR Anti-Trafficking in Persons Legal Framework and International Anti-Trafficking in Persons Standards
	Hong Kong SAR Anti-Trafficking in Persons Legal Framework:


According to Article 3(a) of the “Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons, especially Women and Children, supplementing the United Nations Convention against Transnational Organised Crime” (Palermo Protocol), trafficking in persons (TIP) is defined as:


“The recruitment, transportation, transfer, harbouring or receipt of persons, by means of the threat or use of force or other forms of coercion, of abduction, of fraud, of deception, of the abuse of power or of a position of vulnerability or of the giving or receiving of payments or benefits to achieve the consent of a person having control over another person, for the purpose of exploitation, which shall include, at a minimum, the exploitation of the prostitution of others or other forms of sexual exploitation, forced labour or services, slavery or practices similar to slavery, servitude or the removal of organs.”


Hong Kong SAR addresses TIP through various pieces of local legislation. The conduct of TIP as defined in the Palermo Protocol is covered and effectively prohibited by various pieces of legislation.


The Crimes Ordinance (Cap. 200) 
Prohibits the trafficking of persons to or from Hong Kong SAR for - 
                                                         * the purpose of prostitution; 
                                                         * harbouring another person or exercising control or direction over another person for the purpose of that person’s prostitution or that that person shall do unlawful sexual acts with others; 
                                                         * and any person from procuring another person to become a prostitute or cause prostitution of that person in Hong Kong SAR or elsewhere. 
The Crimes Ordinance also prohibits other crimes including rape, procuring another person by threats to do unlawful sexual acts with others and criminal intimidation. 
Moreover, there are provisions under the Crimes Ordinance that provide extra-territorial effect against certain sexual offences committed against children outside Hong Kong SAR, including related arrangements and advertisements, making them punishable in Hong Kong SAR.


The Human Organ Transplant Ordinance (Cap. 465) 
Prohibits commercial dealings in human organs.










Prevention of Child Pornography Ordinance (Cap. 579) 
Prohibits printing, making, producing, reproducing, copying, importing or exporting, publishing and possessing child pornography.


Immigration Ordinance (Cap. 115) 
Prohibits arrangement for an unauthorised entrant to Hong Kong SAR and employing illegal workers;


Employment Ordinance (Cap. 57) 
Imposes criminal liability on employers involved in non-payment, under-payment of wages or delay in payment of wages, failure to grant rest days and statutory holidays to employees.


Other relevant ordinances 
Prohibit such crimes as assault, forcible taking or detention of persons with intent to sell him or her, child abduction, deception and blackmail, etc.


The most serious penalty for the above offences is life imprisonment. This ‘multiple-legislation’ approach provides law enforcement agencies (LEAs) and prosecutors with more flexibility in investigating and prosecuting TIP cases.
The UN Migration Agency, acting as the secretariat for the Hong Kong SAR Civil Society Anti-Trafficking Task Force, also launched a Handbook on Initial Victim Identification and Assistance for Trafficked Persons. This is a major step in anti-trafficking efforts, as it identifies concrete guidelines and resources for front-line social services, anti-trafficking, labour and other organisations. The handbook is aimed at a number of people including government agencies, NGOs, public health professionals, social workers, lawyers and others who could encounter victims during their work. Additionally, 1037 potential victims underwent preliminary screening and a further 63 were identified as victims of trafficking. 




International Anti-Trafficking in Persons Standards:


Human trafficking is defined as “the recruitment, transport, transfer, harbouring or receipt of a person by such means as threat or use of force or other forms of coercion, abduction, fraud or deception for the purpose of exploitation.”


The Recommended Principles and Guidelines on Human Rights and Human Trafficking was developed by the Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights. It provides practical, rights-based approach policy guidance on the prevention of trafficking and the protection of trafficked persons with a view to facilitate the integration of a human rights perspective into national, regional and international anti-trafficking laws, policies and interventions.


There are several guidelines or steps that are to be taken for the protection of human rights and the prevention of human trafficking.

                                                         * Promotion and protection of human rights;
                                                         * Identification of trafficked persons and traffickers;
                                                         * Research, analysis, evaluation and dissemination;
                                                         * Ensuring an adequate legal framework;
                                                         * Ensuring an adequate law enforcement response;
                                                         * Protection and support for trafficked persons;
                                                         * Preventing trafficking;
                                                         * Special measures for the protection and support of child victims of trafficking;
                                                         * Access to remedies;
                                                         * Obligations of peacekeepers, civilian police and humanitarian and diplomatic personnel; and
                                                         * Cooperation and coordination between States and regions.


Source: Recommended Principles and Guidelines on Human Rights and Human Trafficking.

(7) Money Laundering Activity on Facebook


Money Laundering: 
Money laundering is the processing of funds obtained from crimes into legitimacy, concealing the identity, source or destination of the illegally gained money. In this way it drives forward the crimes and illegal activities into continuation; in this case, it funds the further human trafficking of more victims.


In Hong Kong SAR, 'money laundering' covers all procedures to change the identity of illegally obtained money so that it appears to have originated from a legitimate source. This may include -
                                                         * concealing the true ownership and origin of the money;
                                                         * controlling the money;
                                                         * changing the form of the money.


  



Hong Kong SAR has several pieces of legislation to address the issue.


Relevant to this case, the Organized and Serious Crimes Ordinance (OSCO) which was modelled on the Drug Trafficking (Recovery of Proceeds) Ordinance (DTROP) was brought into operation in December 1994. It extends the money laundering offence to cover the proceeds of indictable offences in addition to drug trafficking.


In 1995, further amendments tightened the money laundering provisions and there is now a significant duty to report suspicious transactions. There is also now a clear statutory obligation to disclose knowledge or suspicion of money laundering transactions.


                                                         * Section 25(1) of the DTROP and OSCO define money laundering as the offence of dealing with any property, knowing or having reasonable grounds to believe it in whole or in part directly or indirectly represents the proceeds of an indictable offence. The offence carries a maximum sentence of 14 years' imprisonment and a maximum fine of HK$5 million.


                                                         * Section 25A(1) imposes a statutory duty on a person, who knows or suspects that any property in whole or in part directly or indirectly represents the proceeds of an indictable offence, or was or is intended to be used in that connection, to make a disclosure to an authorised officer.


                                                         * Section 25A(7) makes it an offence for a person to fail to make such a money laundering disclosure. The offence carries a maximum penalty of a fine at level 5 (at present $25,001 to $50,000) and imprisonment for 3 months.


                                                         * S25(4) OSCO provides that the indictable offence in section 25 and 25A includes a reference to conduct which would constitute an indictable offence if it had occurred in Hong Kong SAR; it shall be an offence for a person to deal with the proceeds of crime or fail to make the necessary disclosure under S25A(1) even if the principal crime is not committed in Hong Kong SAR provided that it would constitute an indictable offence if it had occurred in Hong Kong SAR.




Scale of Money Laundering and Negative Impact:
For a long time, Hong Kong SAR has enjoyed the reputation of good governance and transparent finance in a region that often struggles with corruption and illegal money. It is a reputation the city wants to keep.


But the very strengths that give the city economic dynamism — such as openness to financial flows and the sheer size of the banking system — can also be weaknesses.


The government’s Hong Kong SAR Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing Risk Assessment Report rated Hong Kong SAR’s overall money laundering risk, threat and vulnerability as “medium-high,” citing its strong legal system, political commitment to the issue, and cooperation between the public and private sectors. However the banking sector specifically faces a “high” risk.


Money laundering in Hong Kong SAR is a considerable problem due to the vulnerability created by low taxes, a simplified tax system, and a sophisticated banking system. Laundering channels include Hong Kong SAR’s banking system, as well as its legitimate and underground remittance and money transfer networks.


Hong Kong SAR actively prosecutes money laundering from domestic offences. It also prioritises the confiscation of proceeds of crime through strong action by law enforcement agencies and prosecutors. Authorities respond effectively to the large number of requests for cooperation from foreign counterparts. However, given the jurisdiction’s position as an international financial centre, and the risks it faces from crimes committed abroad, it is not making sufficient outgoing requests for cooperation.  Authorities must focus more on prosecuting the laundering of proceeds from foreign offences.

	Hong Kong SAR Organized and Serious Crimes Ordinance 
Hong Kong SAR addresses Organized Crime and Money Laundering through various pieces of local legislation. Both money laundering and terrorist financing are criminal offenses under the Laws of Hong Kong SAR. According to the Drug Trafficking (Recovery of Proceeds) Ordinance (Cap. 405) and the Organized and Serious Crimes Ordinance (Cap. 455), a person commits the offense of money laundering if he deals with any property, including money, which he knows or has reasonable grounds to believe to be proceeds of drug trafficking or any other indictable offence such as Human Trafficking.
The conduct of Organized Crime as defined in the Convention against Transnational Organized Crime and the activities related to it (including money laundering) are covered and effectively prohibited by local legislation.
The Organized and Serious Crimes Ordinance (OSCO) (Cap. 455)


The Organized and Serious Crime Ordinance is designed to deal with organized crime at both a national and transnational level. It was enacted to create new powers of investigation; Provide for confiscation of proceeds of crime; Enhance sentencing of certain offenders; and Create new offences of dealing with proceeds of crime. It also prohibits dealing with property known or believed to represent proceeds of indictable offence.
Any indictable offence can constitute a predicate offence under OSCO. Section 25(4) further clarifies that references to an indictable offence include a reference to conduct that would constitute an indictable offence if it had occurred in Hong Kong SAR.
                  Money Lenders Ordinance (Cap. 163)
Prohibits lending money at an excessive interest rate. This is an indictable offense.
Crimes Ordinance (Cap. 200)
Prohibits threatening a person with intent, threats to destroy or damage property and procurement of person by threats.
Theft Ordinance (Cap. 210)
Prohibits obtaining property or a pecuniary advantage by deception.

d. Case 3: Mainstar Credit Service Limited
	Summary of Case:
Mainstar Credit Service Limited (“Mainstar”) is a money lender organization in Hong Kong SAR, which grants loans carrying excessive interest rates and uses Facebook as a tool to attract and deceive victims as well as to intimidate and harass them to demand repayment of illegal loans. Law enforcement authorities are actively investigating Mainstar. A number of Mainstar victims are seeking legal remedies which may involve Facebook as a facilitator of illicit activities which have caused significant damages to thousands of migrant workers.


How Facebook enables Mainstar´s illicit business activities: 
                                                         * Mainstar's main business front was their Facebook page.
                                                         * Mainstar would advertise their services, have referrals and acquire clients through Facebook: through either posts, outreach or other pages as well as groups.
                                                         * They would then communicate with their clients through Facebook messages, collect their personal information - including names, photos, contact information, addresses and identity documents - to process for their loan.
                                                         * Facebook would continue to be used for chasing up victims for repayments, often with harassment and threats to the victim and their close ones.
                                                         * Sometimes OWA would subsequently harass friends and family members for the victim's loan through Facebook.

Their personal information including identity cards and passports would be posted without permission, along with slander, harassment or threats.


Legal Implications:


Violations of the Money Lenders Ordinance
Legal Violations:


                                                         1. Mainstar has dealt with the proceeds of an indictable offence by collecting funds subjected to excessive interest loans, contrary to s 24(1) of the Money Lenders Ordinance (MLO). Section 24(4) of the MLO provides that ‘any person who commits an offence under this section shall be liable (a) on summary conviction to a fine of $500,000 and to imprisonment for 2 years; (b) on conviction on indictment to a fine of $5,000,000 and to imprisonment for 10 years.’ 


                                                         2. Mainstar imposes excessive interest rates by granting loans with mandatory ‘processing’ fees. Such fees, according to Section 2 of the MLO, shall constitute interest as they are imposed in excess of the principal. This was confirmed in the recent judgement of Skyline Credit Ltd v Leung Hing Chung [2019] HKCFI 169. 


                                                         3. Mainstarhas failed to procure a copy of loan agreements to borrowers upon request. This constitutes an offence under Section 29 (6). 
                                                         1. Further, according Section  19(4) if the lender failed to procure a copy of a loan agreement within 1 month it is not entitled to sue for recovery of any sum due under the agreement and interest shall not be chargeable. Despite not having responded to the demands of its clients to procure a copy of a loan agreement, Mainstar continuously harassed borrowers for repayment through private messaging, phone calls, Facebook posts and private chats. Mainstar has therefore violated Section 19 (4)


                                                         4. Mainstar has taken photographs of borrowers as collateral for loans, and posted    photographs  on Facebook as  threats  and  to  demand  payment  (see Examples of Facebook´s Involvement​). This contravenes Section 12(c) of the MLR, which is an offence under Section 29(5) of the MLO.


Violations of the Serious Crimes Ordinance (Cap. 200) and the Licensing Condition 10 ©


Legal Violation: 


                                                         1. Licensing Condition 10(c) provides that money lenders and debt collectors shall not “​harass anyone, adopt unlawful or improper debt collection practices.” Mainstar has harassed borrowers via:
                                                         1. Excessive and intimidating text messages and phone calls consisting of swearing, abusive language and shouting;
                                                         2. Threatening letters sent to the employers of the borrower and other third parties;
                                                         3. Publication of the borrower’s personal data online (contrary to DPP 3); 
                                                         4. Disclosure of the borrower’s financial and personal information to employers(DPP 3);
                                                         5. Threats of home visits without warning. 


These actions may amount to criminal intimidation under Section 24 of the Crimes Ordinance. By threatening borrowers, Mainstar intends to coerce them to repay their loans which is an act they are “not legally bound to do”. This is because the loans are charged with excessive interest and Mainstar has failed to provide loan documents, rendering them unenforceable.


                                                         2. Alternatively, these actions may constitute common law harassment. The term “harassment” has been defined as “a course of conduct by a person, whether by words or action, directly or through third parties, sufficiently repetitive in nature as would cause, and which he ought reasonably to know would cause, worry, emotional distress or annoyance to another person.” It is reasonable that excessive phone calls, 5 text messages, threats and public humiliation would adversely affect the victims’ emotions and mental well-being, thus constituting harassment.


                                                         3. Under the Organized and Serious Crimes Ordinance (Cap. 455), a person commits the offence of money laundering if he deals with any property, including money, which he knows or has reasonable grounds to believe to be proceeds of an indictable offence. As  excessive interest, operating an unlicensed savings account and demanding repayment through criminal intimidation constitute indictable offences, the behaviour of Mainstar fits the definition of money laundering as set forth within the Organized and Serious Crimes Ordinance


Violations of the Licensing Conditions 6 and 10(b) and the Personal Data (Privacy) Ordinance (Cap. 486)


Legal Violations


Licensing Conditions 6 and 10 require all money lenders to comply with the PDPO. While Condition 10(b) requires general compliance, Condition 6 requires money lenders specifically to obtain consent in their collection and use of personal data.


Mainstar has taken photographs of borrowers as collateral for loans. This contravenes DPP1, as the personal data collected is not for a lawful purpose, since taking photographs as security for a loan is illegal. Mainstarhas also posted 6 photographs of borrowers on social media, especially Facebook, to threaten them, violating DPP 3 as the borrowers did not consent to their photographs, along with their full names and other personal data, to be posted on social media.


Mainstar has harassed borrowers via excessive text messages and phone calls to them and their employers without consent. This contravenes DPP (3), which states that personal data collected, in this case phone numbers, cannot be used for new purposes without the consent of data subjects, that is, the borrowers.

	b. Corporate Social Responsibility 
	Background:
Stakeholders all over the world are affected by Facebook's social media business. To satisfy the interests of stakeholders, the company's corporate social responsibility (‘CSR’) efforts must meet global standards. The following stakeholder groups are important for Facebook’s CSR strategy:


                                                            * Users/Members 
                                                            * Advertisers
                                                            * Employees
                                                            * Governments
                                                            * Communities


Global governance and law-making on corporate social responsibility (hereafter “CSR”) have known a lot developments throughout the years. In 2011 the UN Guiding Principles (hereafter “UNGPs”) came about that contain principles with a mixture of restating international obligations and codifying social expectations. The UNGPs are non-legally binding and contain social expectations. 


The structure of modern multinational corporations has the fundamental problem that the law is treating every single company separately. With regard to the due diligence approach of CSR in corporate groups there has been some progress in the UNGPs and the French Devoir de Vigilance contains obligations to monitor the supply-chain. The major contribution of the second pillar in the evolution of CSR on a global level has been the codification of a responsibility of corporations and their subsidiaries and suppliers towards respecting human rights. The responsibility to respect human rights does not apply only to the corporation itself but is extended to actions which are directly linked to their operations, products or services by their business relationships. If leverage exists over the linked enterprise it first should try to leverage the contractual partner to uphold human rights standards. In lack of leverage, this should be increased and exercised. If this is impossible, the contractual relationship should be ended. Businesses have to stop believing that what happens at another level, subsidiary or supplier, is not of concern to them, they will become liable for it in some cases.


The duty of care - following the cases of Vedanta and Okpabi - can be established through different "routes" or different types of evidence, these being 1° management control, 2° advice, 3° corporate policies, 4° public commitments. If we would apply these to supply chains: 1° intervention into supplier production, 2° advice/training of suppliers, 3° supplier codes of conduct, 4° public commitment to responsible supply-chain management. If an enterprise published its supplier code of conduct on its website and in its annual report, under the Okpabi criteria, such a statement could in principle be interpreted as public commitments by which a company holds itself out to exercising control over their suppliers and thus give rise to a duty of care.

                                                            1. Securities Exchange Act of 1934
	

The Securities Exchange Act 1934 ('SEA') governs securities transactions after issue on the secondary market. It ensures greater financial transparency, accuracy and overall investor confidence whilst reducing the risks of fraud or manipulation.


Such transactions are -


                                                            * carried on in large volumes by the public generally and in large part originate outside the States in which the exchanges and over-the-counter markets are located and/or are effected by means of the mails and instrumentalities of interstate commerce;
                                                            * constitute an important part of the current of interstate commerce;
                                                            * involve in large part the securities of issuers engaged in interstate commerce;
                                                            * involve the use of credit, directly affect the financing of trade, industry and transportation in interstate commerce, and directly affect and influence the volume of interstate commerce; and affect the national credit.


Frequently the prices of securities on such exchanges and markets are susceptible to manipulation and control, and the dissemination of such prices gives rise to excessive speculation, resulting in sudden and unreasonable fluctuations in the prices of securities which


                                                            1. cause alternately unreasonable expansion and unreasonable contraction of the volume of credit available for trade, transportation, and industry in interstate commerce,
                                                            2. hinder the proper appraisal of the value of securities and thus prevent a fair calculation of taxes owing to the United States and to prevent a fair calculation of taxes owing to the United States and to the several States by owners, buyers, and sellers of securities, and
                                                            3. prevent the fair valuation of collateral for bank loans and/or obstruct the effective operation of the national banking system and Federal Reserve System.


All companies listed on the stock exchange - including Facebook in this case - must follow these requirements. One of these primary requirements includes disclosure requirements.


Facebook must make routine disclosures to the public by filing reports with the SEC. 


This includes -


                                                            * Reporting Company Initial Statement. Facebook is to make an initial disclosure of information concerning the operations, equity structure and securities issued by the company.


                                                            * Annual Report. Detailed annual reports are to be made to the SEC as well as security holders, containing all relevant operational data, an explanation of company performance, an audited financial statement, and detailed information about corporate officers and directors.


                                                            * Quarterly Reports. Contains similar information to the Annual Report, but is made on a quarterly basis rather than an annual basis.


                                                            * Special Reports. Any major operational, structural, financial or ownership changes in the company must be disclosed to the SEC as well as shareholders within a reasonable time of the occurrence.


Any information disclosed must be validated as accurate by corporate executives.


According to the Securities Exchange Commission (SEC), fraud claims filed under Section 10(b)(5) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 may involve some aspects of fraud, deception, misrepresentation, non-disclosure or omission of material facts related to the purchase or sale of a security. In general, SEC anti-fraud regulations provide that an investor may recover damages through a claim of fraud when the investment recommendations made:
                                                            * was based on a misrepresentation or omission of a material fact;
                                                            * was intentional, reckless;
