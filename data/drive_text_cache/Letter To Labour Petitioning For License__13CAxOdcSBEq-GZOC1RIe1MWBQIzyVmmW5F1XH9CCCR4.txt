25 March 2020
PETITION


We, the undersigned, are gravely concerned with the longstanding and serious breaches of Hong Kong laws by Talent (Kenya) Company Limited (“Talent Kenya HK”), the operations of which are not in line with inter alia the legislative requirements and labour standards that employment agencies in Hong Kong are required to follow. Accordingly, in line with your mandate and powers under the Employment Ordinance (Cap. 57), we request that an investigation into Talent Kenya HK be carried out immediately. We further seek: (i) the revocation of the employment agency license of Talent Kenya HK; (ii) the refusal of any renewal for an employment agency licence by Talent Kenya HK; and (iii) that Talent Kenya HK and its ‘associates’ be barred from obtaining an employment agency licence in future as they are not fit and proper to carry on business as an employment agency. 


We enclose herewith brief introductions of our respective organizations for your consideration.

Basic Summary of the Kenyan Cases: 
The basic case typology can be seen in the attached "Kenya FDW Agency TIP Scheme" PDF document. We don't have an exact number of victims, but we can confirm that dozens of women have been brought to HK by this agency from Kenya. It is possible that 200 or more were involved over the past 2 years, but we cannot get the HK government or Kenyan consulate to confirm. We submitted an Access to Information request to the ImmD and they claim: "statistics of entry visa applications for employment as foreign domestic helpers in Hong Kong received from nationals of Nepal and Kenya are not available."
Since you are familiar with the challenges FDWs face, the basic pattern will be familiar to you: prospective workers are lied to in a developing country; an unscrupulous agency overcharges them and forces them to execute various loan and guarantee documents; FDWs are sent to HK where they have their passports taken; the workers have a large percentage of their salary deducted each month until the loan is paid off; abuse and other challenges ensue. 
In other words, same song, different verse. But this case stands out in my mind for a few reasons: 
         * Egregiousness: The case has all the normal bad stuff, it's just worse (collectively) than I've seen in a while, maybe ever. The typical amount the FDWs have to pay is HK$24,000, which is significantly higher than most FDWs are paying these days. And the salary deduction is close to 90% for the first 6 months of employment. Even when Indonesians are charged $24,000 (which I don't think has happened consistently for years) their loans were amortized over 8 or 9 months. In these Kenyan cases, the workers are left with basically nothing. And the Kenyan workers had their passports confiscated, which unfortunately isn't unique. But beyond that, these women were almost completely cut off from the outside world. Most had their phones confiscated, and were not allowed to go outside for the first few months of employment. They were given no days off, and no holidays. And the employers, as you would expect, seem to be uniformly bad, with some being physically and verbally abusive. As a result of all this, an unusually high percentage of these women have been identified as human trafficking victims by the IOM. In fact, I think more than half of the HK gov's confirmed trafficking cases in 2019 were from these Kenyans (can't really confirm that, but we could ask the gov). The concentration of bad things happening to a subset of people provides a cohesive story line, and the fact that the government hasn't really done much to help them, and in most cases has actively fought against and tried to prosecute them, just provides a very clean narrative. 

         * Geography: We think the geography of this story is unique and therefore interesting. This is the first time, to my knowledge, that a group of domestic workers has been brought from Africa (other than Madagascar). This plays a role in several areas, but in particular because these women stand out so much in HK. Unlike Indonesians and Filipinas, these women don't exactly blend in. Why would that matter? As we will share later, many of these women were taken to open bank accounts at Bank of China, where the bank employee only addressed the EA owner (Ivy), who facilitated the opening of their bank accounts. As a possible side note, we do think that with the Belt and Road initiative, there is a chance that exploitation of Africans could increase. Not sure if that's relevant here or not, though. 
            * FWIW, and as a side note: we can show images from FB, etc., revealing a bait-and-switch false marketing on the Kenyan side that lured the women to HK. FB posts incorrectly stated that HK had the highest salaries in Asia for domestic jobs, they can get 10 year employment contracts, etc. 

               * Finance and Money Laundering Elements: A lot has been made by the HK gov and finance industry about improvements to victim identification and AML policies. But this case had red flags everywhere, and yet banking officials didn't do anything to spot, question, or help these women. As a result, we are confident that multiple finance laws were broken - including document forgery, theft, and money laundering - and it is possible that someone within BOC colluded with the EA owner. We can't confirm that yet, but we can prove that regulations were not followed, that document signatures clearly did not match up, and the women will share that the bank officials didn't really talk to them or explain anything to them. If this happened once or twice, it would be strange but maybe not completely noteworthy. But since Ivy did this with many different women, BOC employees should have seen what was going on. This is particularly true since several of these women closed their accounts within only a few months after opening them, which is obviously a red flag. And I will reiterate that these women stand out in HK. There is no way the BOC employees could have overlooked this without, at best, willful ignorance. And FWIW, we hope to get bank footage (of ATM transactions, for example) that can confirm that Ivy stole their money using their ATM cards. We know it happened, but we want to request for the footage to confirm it. 
               * Colorful Characters: The people involved in this story are really interesting. The Kenyan ladies are funny, educated, determined, confident, and well spoken. The agency owner is apparently an alcoholic and possibly has mental health issues. 

               * Policy Backdrop and Gov Inaction (and even adversarial actions): 
No
 Conclusive Law Enforcement Action: Despite this
 case being reported to the Employment Agencies Administration (EAA), Hong Kong Police Force (HKPF), and Immigration (ImmD) in September 2018 (and many times after that), no concrete action has been taken to stop the agency from this illicit behavior or enforce
 the relevant Ordinances. Since September 2018, at least 8 Kenyan nationals have reported this case to the EAA, but the EAA has yet to conduct a thorough investigation, initiate prosecution, or even issue a warning letter - as a result the agency continues
                  *  to operate unlawfully and with almost complete impunity.
                  * Action Plan to fight Trafficking: We think it might make sense to use HK's Action Plan to Combat Trafficking (the Action Plan) as the policy backdrop for the story. The HK government enacted it in 2018 and Matthew Cheung has publicly stated that most, if not all, of the Action Plan elements have been enacted. As a result, the US gov raised HK to a Tier 2 country on its annual TIP report. But did anything actually change? Well, we think that these Kenyan cases provide the clearest examples we have seen to confirm that HK's Action Plan to Combat Trafficking is total BS. From the "victim screening mechanism," the "joint investigation protocol," the increased penalties, the supposed witness immunity, etc., are all exposed. These women have met opposition at pretty much every step of the way in HK. 
                  * For example, when victims explained to the police that Ivy forged some of their employment documents, they were investigated and threatened with arrest. Immigration officials were extremely harsh with them, even after they were identified as victims of trafficking (and tried to coerce them into speaking without their lawyer present, even though the lawyer was on her way). The police were awful to them. In fact, when the first victim reported the abuse, etc., the police officer taking her statement said he lived in the same building as the Kenyan and her employer, he knew the employer's family, and he called the employer and tried to convince the worker to return to the home (mind you, she had been physically assaulted by the employer, which the police officer knew).
                  * And since the first case was reported in Sept 2018, very little has happened against the perpetrator of the crimes. In fact, her employment agency license is up for renewal in April 2020.
