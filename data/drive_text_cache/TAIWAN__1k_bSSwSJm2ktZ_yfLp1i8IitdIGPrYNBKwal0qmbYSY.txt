Money Laundering:
* The loans carry excessive interest rates under Philippines and Taiwan law. Victims are not told and are unaware of the true interest rate of the loan. The loan monies constitute proceeds of an indictable offence.
* Dealing with the proceeds; the lenders provide victims with payment slips and instructs victims to pay in cash at 7-Eleven convenience stores using the QR codes printed. 


Human Trafficking:


Human Trafficking Prevention Act Article 2
	1. Human trafficking:


(1) To recruit, trade, take into bondage, transport, deliver, receive, harbor, hide, broker, or accommodate a local or foreign person, by force, threat, intimidation, confinement, monitoring, drugs, hypnosis, fraud, purposeful concealment of important information, illegal debt bondage, withholding important documents, making use of the victim’s inability, ignorance or helplessness, or by other means against his/her will, for the intention of subjecting him/her to sexual transactions, labor to which pay is not commensurate with the work duty, organ harvesting; or to use the above-mentioned means to impose sexual transactions, labor to which pay is not commensurate with the work duty, or organ harvesting on the victims.
(2) To recruit, trade, take into bondage, transport, deliver, receive, harbor, hide, broker, to sex transactions, labor to which pay is not commensurate with the work duty, or organ harvesting, or to subject people under 18 years of age to sexual transactions, labor to which pay is not commensurate with the work duty, or organ harvesting.


2. Offenses of human trafficking:
Referring to acts of human trafficking conducted in violation of this Act, the Criminal Code, Labor Standard Law, or Child and Youth Sexual Exploitation Prevention Act, or other related laws.


3. Improper debt bondage:
Referring to the use of unclear contracts or unreasonable payments of a debt to place people under bondage, subjecting them to sexual transactions, labor exploitation, or organ harvesting in order to fulfill or guarantee the payment of their debts.


	

* Workers are recruited by agencies in the Philippines. These agencies use debt bondage to exploit migrant workers after they recruit them. 
* These agencies offer job placement opportunities in Taiwan. The agencies charge expensive placement fees which the workers cannot afford; they are then told to take out a loan from a Philippines based money lender in order to pay the fees. These interest rates are excessive and illegal under both Taiwan and Phillipines law, and the true interest rate is not told to the workers. This results in the worker having to work for a long time in order to work off the loan. 


Other Predicate Offences:
* Excessive Interest Rates: Charging and collecting excessive interest rates that are above 20% 
* Money laundering: (continued dealings with loan monies that exceed the legal interest rate) and 
* Fraud: (as the Philippines money lender and the creditor jointly demanded loan payments even though the loan was assigned to the creditor). 


Other Serious Offenses:
* Intimidation: Under intimidation and coercion by agencies, borrowers helplessly surrender numerous signed checks which the agency maintains custody over. The agency then threatens to bounce these checks if borrowers fail to pay the loans. Such conduct is not consented to in any way by borrowers.
